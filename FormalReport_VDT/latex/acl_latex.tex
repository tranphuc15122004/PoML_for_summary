\title{When Does GRPO Help Budget-Controlled Summarization? Content-Gated Post-Training for Vietnamese 4B Models}

\author{Anonymous ACL submission}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Controllable summarization requires a model to preserve reference content while obeying explicit length and sentence budgets. We ask when lightweight post-training can improve this quality--control trade-off, and when group-relative policy optimization (GRPO) is useful beyond supervised instruction tuning. Our method is a reproducible recipe: instruction-conditioned LoRA-SFT, SFT-initialized group-relative updates, and a content-gated safeguard package.
On the single-document test sets VietNews and WikiLingua, the final \texttt{SFT + GRPO v5} systems substantially improve over their raw backbones and compare favorably to several historical baselines; the Instruct system reaches 29.62 ROUGE-2 on VietNews, and the Base system reaches 26.35 on WikiLingua. Historical VietAI, GPT, Qwen3-14B, and other baselines are retained as a clearly separated contextual benchmark until protocol-matched generations are available.
We further audit the failure modes targeted by the safeguard package, including repetition loops and punctuation padding. The task uses reference-derived budgets, so the study does not claim arbitrary user-budget control; prompts, generations, and the baseline-evaluation contract are released for reproducibility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Controllable summarization is useful when systems must meet strict output budgets, such as headline generation, short mobile previews, or template-bound reporting.
In these settings, a model must preserve salient content while also satisfying an explicit length target.
That combination is still difficult for lightweight open-weight language models, especially in Vietnamese, where controllable summarization resources and post-training analyses remain limited.

This paper studies a practical post-training recipe as a controlled empirical case study, not as a proposal for a new optimizer. The two instantiated systems use Qwen3-4B Base and Instruct backbones with instruction-conditioned LoRA-SFT followed by a custom group-relative update.

The central problem is to improve lexical content overlap and budget adherence together. We therefore ask four questions: (RQ1) how the final systems compare with raw controls and established external baselines; (RQ2) how much each post-training stage contributes; (RQ3) whether GRPO benefits from an SFT warm start; and (RQ4) whether the reward and decoding safeguards reduce constraint-driven degeneration.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it presents a transparent content-gated SFT-to-GRPO recipe for Vietnamese budget-controlled summarization. Second, it provides a complete stage and initialization matrix with content, length, and sentence-control metrics under a fixed evaluation contract. Third, it separates direct project evidence from historical contextual baselines and package-level reward-robustness evidence, making the comparison boundary explicit rather than conflating incompatible runs.


\section{Related Work}
\paragraph{Vietnamese summarization benchmarks.}
Vietnamese summarization has moved from benchmark construction toward stronger modeling, but the literature is still comparatively small.
VNDS established an early Vietnamese news summarization benchmark, and WikiLingua added a multilingual how-to benchmark that includes Vietnamese \citep{nguyen2019vnds,ladhak2020wikilingua}.
Modeling papers then showed that pretrained Vietnamese seq2seq backbones matter: ViT5 improved Vietnamese abstractive summarization, and VieSum/BRIO reported further gains from BRIO-style training on a Vietnamese summarization corpus \citep{phan2022vit5,lam2023viesum}.
In this line of work, however, the main objective is still generic summary quality, not explicit control over length or sentence budget.

\paragraph{Controllable and length-aware summarization.}
Controllable summarization has progressed from architectural length conditioning to prompt-based instruction following.
Early work controlled output length through decoder modifications or positional encodings, and later work generalized control to keywords or natural-language prompts \citep{kikuchi2016controlling,fan2018controllable,takase2019positional,he2020ctrlsum,chan2021cmdp}.
More recent systems tighten the length problem further by targeting exact token or sentence counts and by benchmarking instruction-controllable summarization directly \citep{miculicich2023precise,liu2023instrusum}.
Prompt-based multi-control methods and instruction-based length control show that LLMs can follow structured control text, but they also show that precise adherence is still fragile, especially outside English or under multiple simultaneous constraints \citep{jie2024lengthcontrol,instructcmp2024}.
This project sits in that gap: it studies reference-conditioned Vietnamese control with coupled length and sentence targets rather than free-form user preference prompting.

\paragraph{Post-training for summarization behavior.}
Our training recipe belongs to the broader post-training line rather than to architecture changes.
LoRA makes adaptation practical by freezing the backbone and learning low-rank adapters, which is attractive when the desired behavior is narrow and the model is large \citep{hu2021lora}.
Summarization work with learned rewards has also shown that optimizing a better reward signal can outperform ROUGE-only training, as in learning from human feedback \citep{stiennon2020summarize,bohm2019betterrewards}.
Recent policy-optimization methods such as GRPO further reduce the memory cost of on-policy alignment and have become common in open-model post-training; in that sense, our custom rollout-reward loop is best viewed as a lightweight adaptation of this broader family rather than a new optimizer \citep{shao2024deepseekmath}.
The contribution here is the application of these ideas to controllable Vietnamese summarization, not a new post-training algorithm.

\paragraph{Reward design and reward hacking.}
Once a model is optimized against a proxy reward, the reward itself becomes a design object.
The AI safety literature uses the terms reward hacking and specification gaming for cases where optimizing the proxy produces superficially successful but substantively bad behavior \citep{amodei2016concrete,skalse2022rewardhacking}.
In summarization, reward-model work has long shown that ROUGE is an imperfect proxy for human judgment, and later analyses of reward-model overoptimization make the same point more sharply: increasing proxy reward can stop correlating with true quality \citep{gao2022scaling}.
This literature motivates the content gate, degenerate-output filter, and multiplicative reward structure used in our GRPO setup, and it also explains why we treat the changes between the pre-safeguard and safeguarded configurations as a package-level mitigation rather than a single-component ablation.

\subsection{Task and Prompt Setting}
Each example consists of a source document $x$, a control instruction $c$, and a reference summary $y^{*}$.
The control instruction specifies a target length and sentence budget derived from the reference summary statistics, so the task is best viewed as \emph{reference-conditioned control} rather than arbitrary user-defined control.
At inference time, the model receives the source and the control instruction and produces $\hat{y}$.

The instruction schema is shared across SFT, GRPO, and evaluation.
All prompts are built by the project's data augmentation pipeline.
Word counts are computed as whitespace-delimited tokens, and sentence counts are computed from end punctuation.
SFT always uses the approximate constraint template family, GRPO rotates across approximate, range, and upper-bound templates to diversify the control language, and evaluation fixes the approximate template family for consistent testing against the frozen artefacts.

\subsection{Task Formulation, Preprocessing, and Prompt Construction}
Each training or evaluation instance is represented as a triplet
$(x, c, y^{*})$, where $x$ is a source document, $c$ is a
control instruction, and $y^{*}$ is a reference summary. The control
instruction is not arbitrary user input; it is derived from $y^{*}$ after
preprocessing, which truncates source and summary fields to the working
context budget and computes target word and sentence counts from the
reference. The task is therefore controllable summarization under
reference-conditioned budgets rather than unconstrained free-form prompting.

The pipeline constructs chat-style prompts rather than plain source-target
pairs. For SFT, each example contains system, user, and assistant turns, and
the control language is instantiated with approximate templates such as
``around $X$ words.'' For GRPO and evaluation, the assistant response is
removed, the reference is stored separately for reward computation or scoring,
and the model must generate a summary that respects the requested control
budget from the system and user turns alone. Across GRPO, the control language
rotates across three template families: approximate constraints, explicit
ranges, and upper bounds; evaluation fixes the approximate
family for consistent testing. This asymmetric prompt
construction is deliberate: Stage 1 teaches the summarization task under a
stable instruction schema, while Stage 2 exposes the policy to broader control
wording without changing the underlying supervision target.

\subsection{Stage 1: Instruction-conditioned LoRA-SFT}
Stage 1 gives the backbone its base summarization and instruction-following
behavior. Each processed $\{source, target\}$ pair is converted into an
instruction-following chat example, so the model learns not only to compress
Vietnamese content but also to map explicit control instructions to the
desired output form. In practice, this stage is responsible for the largest
single improvement in instruction following and summarization quality in the
project, and it produces the checkpoint used to warm-start the GRPO stage.

Both Qwen3-4B families studied in the paper, Base and Instruct, are adapted
with LoRA over the attention and MLP projection layers. All canonical runs use
LoRA with rank $r = 32$, scaling parameter $\alpha = 32$, and dropout $0.05$. The main SFT
runs use a 3,072-token context length, learning rate $5\times10^{-5}$,
warmup ratio 0.1, two epochs, and thinking mode suppression enabled.

\subsection{Stage 2: GRPO Alignment}
Stage 2 is implemented as a custom rollout-and-reward loop rather than
through a stock trainer abstraction. In each training step, the policy formats
a batch of prompts, samples multiple completions per prompt, scores them with
the rule-based reward function, normalizes the rewards within each prompt group, and applies
a PPO-style policy update with KL regularization. The frozen reference model
is the \emph{initial GRPO policy}: for \emph{Fresh} GRPO it is the pretrained
backbone plus a newly attached LoRA adapter at initialization, whereas for
\emph{SFT-initialized} GRPO it is the backbone plus the SFT checkpoint. The
KL term therefore anchors optimization to the exact starting policy rather
than to the raw backbone.

The project studies two initialization modes. \emph{Fresh} GRPO attaches a
new LoRA adapter directly to the pretrained backbone, while
\emph{SFT-initialized} GRPO warm-starts from the SFT checkpoint. In the final
paper narrative, fresh GRPO is treated as an ablation baseline and
SFT-initialized GRPO is treated as the intended deployment recipe.

Within each update, the trainer formats a batch of prompts, samples $K$
completions per prompt, computes rewards $r_{i,k}$, and normalizes them
within each prompt group:
\begin{equation}
A_{i,k} = \frac{r_{i,k} - \bar{r}_{i}}{\max(\sigma_i, 0.01)},
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes prompts and $k$ indexes the sampled completions for prompt $i$.
The policy is then updated with a PPO-style clipped objective over generated
tokens plus a KL penalty to the frozen reference model. The per-token
probability ratio is defined as
\begin{equation}
\rho_{i,k,t} = \exp\bigl(\log \pi_{\theta} - \log \pi_{\text{old}}\bigr),
\end{equation}
and the policy objective with the clipped surrogate is
\begin{equation}
c_{i,k,t} = \operatorname{clip}(\rho_{i,k,t}, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L_{\text{policy}}
&= -\frac{1}{|M|} \sum_{i,k,t}
   \min\bigl(\rho_{i,k,t} A_{i,k},\; c_{i,k,t} A_{i,k}\bigr)\\[-1mm]
&\qquad\qquad {}\times m_{i,k,t},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with the total loss including a KL penalty:
\begin{equation}
L = L_{\text{policy}} + \beta L_{\text{KL}},
\end{equation}
where $m_{i,k,t}$ masks generated tokens up to the first EOS token.
In the implementation, $\pi_{\text{old}}$ is the detached current policy
within the same on-policy step, and the KL term is computed token-wise
against the frozen reference model. Among the stored configurations, the final
systems instantiate this stage with the canonical \texttt{v5} bundle, which
uses $K=8$, learning rate $2\times10^{-6}$, and $\beta=0.04$. This makes the
method a lightweight GRPO-style rollout-and-update loop tailored to the
present summarization setting rather than a wrapper around a stock trainer,
with Stage 2 acting as a control-refinement and failure-mitigation layer on
top of the capabilities learned in Stage 1.

\subsection{Reward Design and Anti-Hacking Safeguards}
The reward combines content quality with explicit control adherence.
Content quality is measured as
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{acc}} = 0.5\,\mathrm{ROUGE\mbox{-}1}_{F1} + 0.5\,\mathrm{ROUGE\mbox{-}L}_{F1}.
\end{equation}
Length and sentence rewards, denoted $R_{\text{len}}$ and $R_{\text{sent}}$,
are computed from the same instruction-specific tolerance rules used to
construct the control prompts in the previous subsection.
The final reward uses a multiplicative gate:
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{total}} = R_{\text{acc}} \times \left(1 + 0.3 R_{\text{len}} + 0.2 R_{\text{sent}}\right),
\end{equation}
which gates the constraint bonus on content overlap: when $R_{\text{acc}}=0$, the
total reward is zero regardless of constraint satisfaction.

Because constraint-based rewards are susceptible to reward hacking, where a
model learns to satisfy length and sentence budgets with garbage content, the
reward function is embedded in a defense-in-depth strategy with three layers.
At the \textbf{generation} layer, the repetition penalty is kept at
$1.0$ and n-gram blocking is disabled (size $0$). Preliminary experiments
showed that higher values of either parameter, when applied by the
HuggingFace decoder over the full $\approx 2{,}000$-token input sequence,
penalized the model for reusing source-article vocabulary and pushed decoding
toward out-of-distribution symbols, which is especially damaging for short
Vietnamese summaries where named entities and key terms must appear verbatim.

At the \textbf{detection} layer, a post-hoc degenerate-output filter
examines each rollout with five sequential
checks: empty or whitespace-only output; single-token blobs longer than $25$
characters without spaces; fewer than three alphabetic characters; alpha
character ratio below $12\%$ when exceeding $15$ characters; and token
repetition where one token accounts for over $60\%$ of all tokens or unique
tokens fall below $30\%$. Any output flagged by this filter receives
$R_{\text{len}}=0$ and $R_{\text{sent}}=0$, so degenerate rollouts cannot
earn any constraint bonus; under the multiplicative gate, only residual
content overlap captured by $R_{\text{acc}}$ can remain.

At the \textbf{formula} layer, the multiplicative gate structurally prevents
constraint-only optimization: no amount of length or sentence adherence can
compensate for zero content quality. Additionally, the upper-bound constraint
``\vn{không quá $X$ từ}'' enforces a floor of $\max(3, 0.15 \times \text{max\_wc})$
words; outputs shorter than this floor receive $R_{\text{len}}=0$, closing
the loophole of satisfying the constraint with one- or two-word garbage.

The stage-wise comparison yields three observations about the evaluated training recipe:

\paragraph{SFT provides the largest stage-wise gain.}
Both pretrained backbones have substantially lower ROUGE-2 than their SFT variants on both primary datasets.
The largest content gains occur after SFT: macro ROUGE-2 rises from $10.34$ to $26.17$ for Base and from $14.67$ to $26.15$ for Instruct.
We therefore treat SFT as the dominant quality stage, with subsequent GRPO interpreted as a refinement of this strong starting point.

\paragraph{Warm-started GRPO improves content and, for Base, control.}
SFT-initialized GRPO v5 raises macro ROUGE-2 from $26.17$ to $28.45$ for Base and from $26.15$ to $28.81$ for Instruct.
For Base, it also reduces mean length distance from $9.96$ to $4.76$ words; for Instruct, SFT starts at $5.48$ words and GRPO changes it slightly to $5.89$ words.
On VietNews, the Base warm-start improves ROUGE-2 from $26.61$ to $28.97$ and length distance from $5.73$ to $1.16$ words.

\paragraph{Fresh GRPO underperforms SFT initialization.}
Applying GRPO directly to the pretrained checkpoint (Fresh GRPO v5, without the intermediate SFT step) underperforms SFT-initialized GRPO on both primary datasets.
As shown by the isolated markers in Figure~\ref{fig:stage-ablation-trajectory}, the Fresh GRPO models reach only $20.73$ and $18.67$ ROUGE-2 on VietNews, compared with $28.97$ and $29.62$ after SFT initialization.
The same ordering holds on WikiLingua ($9.71$/$8.09$ versus $26.35$/$25.59$), supporting SFT warm-starting as the reliable condition for useful GRPO in this study.

\paragraph{Observed trade-offs and backbone choice.}
The trajectory curves show that the two-stage recipe has a genuine trade-off rather than a universal win on every control metric.
Instruct SFT+GRPO v5 has the highest VietNews ROUGE-2 ($29.62$), while Base SFT+GRPO v5 is slightly stronger on WikiLingua ($26.35$ versus $25.59$) and gives the lowest single-dataset length distance on VietNews ($1.16$ words).
SFT alone gives the lowest VietNews length distance for Instruct ($1.09$ words), whereas warm-started GRPO gives the lowest WikiLingua distance for Base ($8.35$ words).
The result supports a conditional-refinement claim: GRPO is most useful after SFT, but its content and control effects depend on the backbone and domain.

\subsection{RQ3: GRPO Initialization and Sentence Control}
The initialization comparison is a separate question from the stage decomposition above. Holding the v5 bundle fixed, SFT-initialized GRPO is consistently stronger than Fresh GRPO: pooled ROUGE-2 is $28.45$ versus $18.53$ for Base and $28.81$ versus $16.55$ for Instruct. The gap is especially large on WikiLingua (Base: $26.35$ versus $9.71$; Instruct: $25.59$ versus $8.09$), while the length-distance gap is also substantial for the Base branch. These paired differences motivate the warm-start recipe, but they do not establish that SFT initialization is the only causal factor because the two branches begin from different learned policies.

\subsection{RQ4: Reward Robustness and Mitigation Package}

Reward hacking occurs when a model optimizes the reward signal without improving output quality.
The following analysis is explicitly historical: its stored v3/v5 generations use the earlier failure-analysis snapshot, whereas the original project evaluation supplies the stage-wise results above.
In v3 (the pre-safeguard bundle), three interacting design flaws created conditions under which this behavior emerged.
First, v3 used a \textbf{linear reward sum}
\[
R_{\text{total}} = w_{\text{acc}}R_{\text{acc}} + w_{\text{len}}R_{\text{len}} + w_{\text{sent}}R_{\text{sent}},
\]
which allowed the constraint terms to contribute independently of content: a model could score $\approx 0.50$ by satisfying length and sentence budgets with garbage while ignoring $R_{\text{acc}}$.
Second, a repetition penalty of $1.3$ was applied by the HuggingFace decoder over the full $\approx 2{,}000$-token input sequence, penalizing the model for reusing any vocabulary from the source article and pushing generation toward out-of-distribution symbols.
Third, n-gram blocking with size $3$ blocked source trigrams, forbidding named entities and key Vietnamese content words.
The resulting failure modes, concentrated in the Base branches where the pretrained backbone lacks the instruction-following prior of the Instruct model, included repetition loops (e.g., \texttt{assistant} repeated $128$ times, $\text{ROUGE-2}=0.0$), punctuation padding ($245$ words of periods, $\text{LenErr}=1189\%$), digit blobs that satisfied ``\vn{không quá $X$ từ}'' with $R_{\text{len}}=1.0$ and $R_{\text{acc}}=0$, and comma-separated token loops carrying no semantic content.
Figure~\ref{fig:reward-cases} illustrates the two most severe modes qualitatively.
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-v4v5} records the historical bundle-level v4-v5 comparison, and Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-nosentence} keeps the sentence-reward ablation that remains inconclusive under the frozen sampling protocol.

The canonical v5 bundle addresses each root cause through the defense-in-depth safeguards described in Section~4.4: the generation-layer fixes restore natural decoding, the detection-layer filter catches degenerate outputs, and the multiplicative gate structurally prevents constraint-only optimization.

Base SFT-init suffered severe length explosion under v3 ($\text{LenErr}=425\%$), which v5 cuts to $14.94\%$ while raising ROUGE-2 from $18.03$ to $26.38$.
The Instruct branches were substantially more robust under v3 (degenerate rates remained below $1\%$), owing to their chat-tuning prior; their v3-to-v5 gains are correspondingly incremental, with Instruct SFT-init v5 reaching the highest mean $R_{\text{acc}}$ ($0.4213$) and ROUGE-2 ($27.65$).
Within that historical snapshot, the v5 safeguard package sharply reduces catastrophic failures for the Base branches and preserves the already-low Instruct degeneration rate. This is supporting evidence for the package, rather than a causal ablation of one detector or hyperparameter.

The historical comparison supports v5 as a stable canonical configuration for the main stage-wise evaluation.
The Instruct branches' near-immunity to degeneration is consistent with the primary finding that an SFT warm-start or equivalent generative prior is useful for effective GRPO in this setting.
The v3-to-v5 improvement is properly understood as the effect of a \textbf{mitigation package} (generation, detection, and formula-level safeguards acting jointly), rather than an ablation of any single component.
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-sentence} retains the sentence-control results that justify keeping sentence reward as an explicit metric, and Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-nosentence} shows that removing that term does not support a causal claim under the current evaluation protocol.

\section{Conclusion}
Controllable Vietnamese summarization is both a content-generation and alignment problem: the model must preserve salient information while obeying explicit length and sentence budgets. This paper studies that problem through a controlled Qwen3-4B post-training comparison.
Across the primary VietNews and WikiLingua evaluation suite, SFT supplies the dominant content gain, SFT-initialized GRPO acts as a conditional refinement, and Fresh GRPO remains clearly weaker than warm-started GRPO. The strongest result is 29.62 ROUGE-2 on VietNews from Instruct SFT+GRPO v5; Base SFT+GRPO v5 reaches 28.97 on VietNews and mean length distances of 1.16 and 8.35 words on VietNews and WikiLingua.
These conclusions are deliberately bounded to reference-conditioned Vietnamese single-document summarization under the stated original evaluation protocol. The study does not establish arbitrary-budget controllability, superiority over external models evaluated under different protocols, or a causal effect for any individual v3--v5 hyperparameter. The results also show that control gains are backbone- and domain-dependent: Instruct SFT already has strong length adherence, while Base benefits substantially from GRPO refinement.

GRPO training and evaluation share the same prompt shape: both keep only the system and user turns, store the gold summary separately as \textit{reference}, and preserve the control metadata in \textit{meta}.
The differences are that GRPO rotates all template families during rollout training, whereas evaluation fixes the approximate template, and SFT is the only stage that includes an assistant turn inside the serialized chat sample.
